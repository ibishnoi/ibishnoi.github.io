PWR in R

By Indra Bishnoi | December 9, 2020

As researchers itâ€™s always tough to gage how many participants we need to recruit for our study. There are a number of ways to figure this out, but R offers some good packages that do this for us.

Let’s take a look at the pwr package. To begin, let’s install and call on this package:

install.packages("pwr")
library(pwr)

Once you’ve got that, let’s take a look at the many statistical analyses this package supports.

pwr functions

pwr gives you the ability to run the power calculations for both one and two sample(s) t-tests using the <pwr.t.test> and <pwr.t2n.test>functions, respectively. Additionally, correlations (<pwr.r.test>), chi-squares (<pwr.chisq.test>), one-way ANOVAs (<pwr.anova.test>), and even general linear models (<pwr.f2.test>) are supported.

Each of these functions have specific arguments that need to be addressed. You’ll find all of these below.

T-test

pwr.t.test(d = , n = , sig.level = , power = )

Here, d is your Cohen’s d/effect size, n is your sample size, sig.level is your alpha (usually .05), and power is the power you’d like to obtain. By filling out all except one, e.g. n, R will return the missing value. The same logic applies to all of the following functions.

If you’d like to use a t-test, but have unequal sample sizes, make sure to use

pwr.t2n.test(n1 = , n2= , d = , sig.level =, power = )

Use n1 and n2 to identify the sample size of each group.

Correlation

pwr.r.test(n = , r = , sig.level = , power = )

Here, n is the sample size, r in the correlation coefficient, sig.level is your alpha, and power is your desired power.

Chi-square

pwr.chisq.test(w =, N = , df = , sig.level =, power = )

Here, w is your effect size, N is the total sample size, df is your degrees of freedom, sig.level is your alpha, and power is your desired power.

One-way ANOVA

pwr.anova.test(k = , n = , f = , sig.level = , power = )

Here, k is your number of groups, n is the sample size, f is the effect size, sig.level is your alpha, and power is your desired power.

General linear model

pwr.f2.test(u =, v = , f2 = , sig.level = , power = )

Here, u and v are the numerator and denominator degrees of freedom, respectively, f2 is the effect size, sig.level is your alpha, and power is your desired power.

Examples

To gain a better understanding of these, let’s run through some examples.

Let’s say we have 5 groups, cat owners, dog owners, bird owners, reptile owners, and no pet owners, and we want to compare their subjective stress levels in an anova. We want to calculate the n (sample size) we would need to informatively compare these groups. We want a standard power of .80 and sig. level of .05, and in the literature we see that there is a moderate effect size (.25) so we stick to that. This is what this example would look like:

pwr.anova.test(k = 5, n = , f = .25, sig.level = .05, power = .80)

Let’s do another example:

Support we want to conduct a goodness of fit test, or chi-squared. Let’s say we’d like to use a moderate effect size (.30), with a significance level of .05 and standard power of .80. We want 5 groups, so a df of 4. But what should our total sample size (N) be? Let’s calculate!

pwr.chisq.test(w = .30, N = , df = 4, sig.level = .05, power = .80)

Examples plotted

In order to plot our power analyses, the pwr package has an amazing plot function that let’s us standardize our plots.

First, save your analyses into the global environment

p.anova.plot <- pwr.anova.test(k = 5, n = , f = .25, sig.level = .05, power = .80)
p.chi.plot <- pwr.chisq.test(w = .30, N = , df = 4, sig.level = .05, power = .80)

Now, use <plot> to plot!

plot(p.anova.plot)
plot(p.chi.plot)

We can use this plotting format for all of the pwr analyses!
